Trintiy College Library: Home to The Book of Kells & The Long Room

The Book of Kells

The Book of Kells is a gospel book, containing the four accounts of Christ’s life that form the New Testament of the Bible. One of the few priceless examples of a significant book-making tradition that flourished in the Irish Church in the eighth and ninth centuries, it is a rare and priceless relic. The manuscript's name comes from the monastery in Ireland's County Meath, Kells. Around 806 AD, monks escaping Viking attacks at the monastery of Iona, off the western coast of Scotland, founded the monastery.

Although it is impossible to date the manuscript with absolute certainty, it is generally agreed that it was probably started on Iona in the late eighth century, and may have been transferred to Ireland for safety prior to its completion. It remained at Kells until the late seventeenth century, when it was brought to Trinity College in Dublin for safekeeping.

Time-Line of The Book of Kells & The Old Library

800AD: The Book of Kells was written on The Island of Iona, off the coast of Scotland. Researchers believe that it was originally produced in a monastery by three artists and four scribes. When Iona was attacked by Vikings, the surviving monks moved to a sister monastery in Kells, Co. Meath. Some scholars believe that it was here that the Book of Kells was finished.

1592: The oldest university in Ireland was established when Queen Elizabeth founded Trinity College Dublin in 1592. Today, the Book of Kells is kept in Trinity's Old Library building from the 18th century, which also houses the 65-meter-long Long Room, one of the most stunning libraries in the world.

1653: The Book of Kells remained in Kells until 1653, when it was transported to Dublin because the town had sustained significant damage as a result of military occupation during the 17th-century wars.

1661-1682: The Book of Kells was given to Trinity College Dublin by the Bishop of Meath between 1661 and 1682 for safe keeping, and it has been kept there since.

1732: The Long Room is located in the Old Library building, which opened its doors in 1732. Since then, the volumes of books housed in The Long Room have served as a working library that is still used by scholars from Ireland and other countries.

1861: Due to an expanding library of books, the original flat Long Room ceiling was discovered to be in danger of collapse. A dark-oak barrel vaulted ceiling was erected and is still in place today, along with an above gallery of bookcases.

2018: The Book of Kells received a record-breaking million visitors in a single year. Cassie Clemans, who came from Oregon in the United States with her husband, was the millionth visitor.

The Old Library

The Library began with the founding of Trinity College in 1592. The building was design by Thomas Burgh 1712 and took 20 years to complete. In 1661, Henry Jones presented it with the Book of Kells, its most famous manuscript. James Ussher (1625–56), Archbishop of Armagh, whose most important works were "Veterum Epistolarum Hibernicarum Sylloge" (1632) and "Brittanicarum Ecclesiarum Antiquitates" (1639), left his valuable library, comprising several thousand printed books and manuscripts, to the Library.

His complete works were published by the Library in twenty-four volumes. In 1801, the Library was given legal deposit rights, making it the only library in Ireland to have such rights for the United Kingdom at that time. the site selected by the creators more than three centuries ago to protect the printed materials from the elements. Over 200,000 of the library's oldest books are housed in the 65-meter-long room, which is the longest single-room library in all of Europe. At each end of the gallery are reading rooms, making it a functioning library. Given the rarity of the space, use and pervation are handled with extra caution.

Redevelopment Project

The Old Library is one of Ireland's most famous landmarks and one of the greatest libraries in the entire world. In its distinctive twin function as a top-notch library and a national cultural institution, it unites legacy and scholarship. For more than 400 years, the Library of Trinity College Dublin has been responsible for caring for its priceless treasures, which span millennia. However, it currently confronts serious environmental and conservation challenges. To protect the Old Library structure and preserve its priceless treasures for future generations, the Old Library Redevelopment Project will make use of the best design and technology of the twenty-first century. It entails immediate structural and environmental improvements as well as the renovation of the facilities to match the top libraries and museums throughout the globe. A top-notch design team is being led by the award-winning architects Heneghan Peng in this game-changing construction.
